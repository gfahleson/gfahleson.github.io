Did the Big Ten Lack in their Performance During 2023 the NCAA Tournamnet?

Big Ten
Basketball
NCAA
Author

Grace Fahleson

Published

November 5, 2024

March used to be a time when Big Ten teams dominated the NCAA tournament. Between 2000 and 2019, the conference sent 16 teams to the Final Four—more than any other conference during that span.

However, this trend has taken a downturn in recent years. In 2021, only one of the Big Ten’s eight tournament teams advanced to the Sweet Sixteen. The 2022 tournament was even more disappointing, with all five Big Ten teams seeded eighth or higher falling short of expectations. 2023 was even more unfortunate for the conference as their top team representing the conference fell to a 16 seed in the first round.

So, what’s behind this decline? Are Big Ten teams underperforming when it matters most, or is this just a streak of bad luck?

bigregular <- regularseason23 %>%
  filter(team_short_display_name %in% big10)

tournament <- c("Maryland", "Illinois", "Indiana", "Iowa", "Michigan St", "Northwestern", "Penn State", "Purdue")

postseason23 <- mbb_team_box %>% filter(game_date > "2023-03-14" & game_date <= "2023-04-03")

bigpost <- postseason23 %>%
  filter(team_short_display_name %in% tournament)

regularthreepct <- bigregular %>% 
  group_by(team_short_display_name) %>% 
  summarise(
    count = n(),
    SeasonThreePct = sum(three_point_field_goal_pct),
    TotalGames = n()
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    ThreePointPct = SeasonThreePct/TotalGames
  )

During the regular season, every Big Ten team managed to shoot above 30% from beyond the arc.

Yet, come NCAA tournament time, four of the conference’s eight teams fell below that threshold. Penn State stood out as the lone exception, improving its three-point shooting from the regular season to the postseason.

Of course, the headline story from this year’s tournament was the shocking upset of top-seeded Purdue by 16th-seeded Fairleigh Dickinson. So, what exactly went wrong for the Boilermakers during March Madness? Let’s break it down.

Code 
bigmbb <- bigregular %>%
  select(team_short_display_name, field_goals_attempted, field_goals_made, field_goal_pct) %>%
  mutate_at(vars(-team_short_display_name), as.numeric) %>%
  group_by(team_short_display_name) %>%
  summarize(
    TotalFG = sum(field_goals_made),
    TotalFGA = sum(field_goals_attempted)
  ) %>%
  mutate(FGPct = TotalFG/TotalFGA) %>%
  rename(Team = team_short_display_name) %>%
  select(Team, FGPct) %>%
  arrange(desc(FGPct))

When the postseason arrived, Purdue’s field goal percentage plummeted by a 10%. Along with a drop in their three-point shooting, this decline in offensive efficiency brought troubles for the Boilermakers.

Offensive struggles weren’t unique to Purdue—six of the Big Ten’s eight tournament teams experienced a decline in shooting compared to their regular-season performance.

With offenses on the edge, the question becomes: did these teams make-up for their scoring struggles with strong defensive play? Let’s take a closer look.

Code 
turnovers <- bigpost %>%
  group_by(team_short_display_name) %>%
  summarise (
    Giveaways = sum(total_turnovers),
    Takeaways = sum(steals)) %>%
rename(Team = team_short_display_name)

ggplot() + 
  geom_dumbbell(
    data=turnovers, 
    aes(y=Team, x=Takeaways, xend=Giveaways),
    size = 2,
    colour = "grey",
    colour_x = "blue",
    colour_xend = "red") +
  labs(
    title="Did Big Ten team's win the turnover battle in the postseason?", 
    subtitle = "No Big Ten team had more steals than turnovers in the tournament",
    caption="Source: hoopR | By Grace Fahleson"
  ) + 
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(
    title = element_text(color = "black", lineheight  =   14, face = "bold"),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(lineheight  =   11),

Big Ten teams failed to win the turnover battle in the NCAA tournament, and when paired with their offensive struggles, it led to yet another disappointing postseason for the conference.

The recent trend of underperformance in March Madness raises a pressing question: is this simply bad luck? The numbers suggest otherwise. With Big Ten teams consistently underperforming in nearly every statistical category during the postseason compared to the regular season, the issues run deeper than just poor luck.

The pressure is on for the conference to reverse this trend. It’s been over two decades since Michigan State delivered the Big Ten’s last national championship in 2000. Can the conference reclaim its former glory? Only time will tell.
